Waves and Change

A wave looks like a single, sweeping force — something with direction and momentum, a thing that moves. But look closer and each particle in the wave is doing almost nothing. It bobs up, then down, tracing a small circle in place. No particle travels with the wave. The wave is an invisible force made visible only because it happens to pass through a medium, and each tiny part of that medium dances a quiet, private rhythm.

This is how change works, too. Everyone who has ever been part of something bigger — a movement, a shift, a slow turning of the world — has mostly just been bobbing. Doing their small thing. The wave is not any one person's contribution. It is what emerges when enough people dance in place at roughly the right time.

Lant Pritchett once said something to a room full of education researchers that stuck with me. He told them: each of you thinks you are contributing a diamond to the pile — that yours is the piece of work that will finally tip the scales. But what each of you is really doing is adding a handful of gravel. And that's not a failure. That's how it works. The pile grows because enough people show up with their handful, and eventually the weight of all that gravel is what moves things.

There is a humility in this, and a strange comfort. You are not the wave. You are not even sure you're doing anything that matters. You are just going up and down. But the wave needs you — needs all of you — because without the medium, there is no wave at all. The force has nowhere to go.
